Executive Summary

The project in brief

WanderScape is a UX research-led mobile app designed to help travelers discover authentic local experiences beyond the typical tourist trail. The project followed a complete UX process over 1 month and 3 weeks — from surveys and user interviews through competitive analysis, wireframing, high-fidelity design, and usability testing.

The core insight driving WanderScape: popular travel apps create a feedback loop that makes tourism more homogeneous, not more personal. By surfacing local-guide recommendations and vibe-based search instead of popularity rankings, WanderScape gives travelers the tools to find the real version of a place.

Core Thesis: The best UX for travel isn't optimizing for the popular — it's making the authentic discoverable.

The Problem
Mainstream travel apps surface the same popular spots to every visitor — creating a feedback loop that crowds out authentic local experiences.
The Research
Surveys and interviews revealed that 75% plan ahead, get inspiration from social media, and want landmark tours, food tours, and city walks — but feel existing apps miss the genuine local side of destinations.
The Solution
A four-tab mobile app (Explore, Wishlist, Map, Profile) with category filtering, featured local experiences, and a calmer blue/white visual design iterated from usability testing.
Recommendations

What comes next

Three directions for WanderScape's next iteration, based on research findings and usability testing.

Recommendation 01 — Local Guide System
Build a verified local guide layer where residents can submit and curate recommendations. Trust in recommendations is the biggest barrier to discovery. Local voices beat algorithms.
Recommendation 02 — AI Personalization
Implement AI-based Explore feed personalization based on saved wishlist items and browsing patterns — surfacing destinations that match each user's interests rather than generic popularity rankings.
Recommendation 03 — Social Sharing
Add wishlist sharing so travelers can share curated lists with friends or publish them publicly. Research showed social platforms are where inspiration starts; WanderScape should be where it lands.

The most important finding from this project wasn't about features — it was about trust. Users don't distrust travel apps because the information is wrong. They distrust them because the information feels optimized for someone else's interests, not theirs.

Key Takeaway: The best UX for discovery isn't the most efficient path to a booking — it's the experience that makes a traveler feel like they found something nobody else knows about. That feeling of discovery is the product.

Key Features

Four screens, one focused experience

WanderScape is organized into four core tabs, each designed to support a distinct phase of how travelers plan and experience a destination — all rooted in what the research revealed about real traveler behavior.

🔍
Explore Screen
A prominent search bar, featured destination cards, and category tabs for Nature, Arts, Food, and Sports. High-quality imagery makes browsing feel exciting and inspiring rather than transactional.
❤️
Wishlist Screen
Clean layout with "My Wishlists" and "Recently Viewed" tabs. Users can create new wishlists from an empty state with a "Start exploring" prompt — addressing the budget planning concern from research.
🗺️
Map Screen
Utility-focused with input fields for start and end locations, real-time transportation details, and map previews. Designed for the 75% of travelers who plan ahead and need reliable navigation tools.
👤
Profile Screen
Personal account management and saved preferences — completing the four-tab bottom navigation that keeps all core features within one tap from anywhere in the app.
📍
Category Filtering
Nature, Arts, Food, and Sports filters on the Explore screen let users quickly narrow to what excites them — reflecting the real variety of tour types identified in user interviews.
✨
Featured Experiences
Highlighted destination cards with high-resolution imagery — from the Ellis Island Museum to Zip Aviation helicopter tours — making discovery feel like scrolling a curated travel feed rather than a booking platform.

Design Solutions

Research → Design

Every major design decision traces back to a specific research finding. Here's how the data shaped the product.

01
Research Finding
Users found original colors too saturated — testing showed they created cognitive noise and pulled attention away from destination imagery.
Design Decision
Calmer blue-and-white palette. Shifted to lower-saturation tones so the UI recedes and destination photography takes center stage — the product is the place, not the app.
02
Research Finding
75% of travelers plan trips in advance. An empty wishlist with no guidance felt like a dead end and discouraged early engagement.
Design Decision
Wishlist empty state with CTA. Added "Start exploring" prompt with a heart-and-plane illustration — giving users a clear next action and framing the wishlist as a planning tool, not a graveyard.
03
Research Finding
Users felt existing apps recommended the same popular spots to everyone, regardless of personal travel style or interests.
Design Decision
4-question onboarding survey. Asks travel style, frequency, activity type, and experience preference on first launch — personalizing the Explore feed before the user sees a single recommendation.
04
Research Finding
Landmark tours, food tours, and city walks were most-requested — users want to filter by experience type, not by a popularity algorithm.
Design Decision
Nature / Arts / Food / Sports category tabs. Four filters mapped directly to what research said people want — making discovery feel intentional rather than algorithmically driven.
