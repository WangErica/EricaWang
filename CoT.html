Overview

A city imagined
125 years from now

City of Tomorrow is set 125 years in the future on Earth. After decades of severe pollution and environmental degradation, the brightest minds came together to engineer a sustainable city in the sky — free from the toxic conditions on the ground.

But sustainability alone wasn't enough. Early in the city's history, the lack of entertainment and leisure led to social unrest. The founders realized a balanced life — with time for community, culture, and joy — was as essential as clean air. This gave rise to the Cultural District, and every building's playful form became a symbolic commitment to that balance.

As my NYU Tandon capstone, I built this world over 15 weeks — from narrative and concept through 3D modeling in Maya, texturing, Arnold rendering, and a live showcase exhibition.

Process

Concept sketch to
rendered world

The project followed a structured 15-week arc — from world-building and narrative to modeling, material refinement, rendering, and a physical showcase presentation. Each week was documented on the project blog.

01
Concept & Narrative
Established the world's backstory — "The Great Shift" — and defined the city's values: sustainability, modular pod living, and playful architecture where buildings take shapes of everyday objects.
02
3D Modeling in Maya
Each building was designed around a symbolic everyday object — a dumbbell, a lipstick, a book, a wine glass — chosen to reflect the district's purpose. See the full architecture meanings below.

Applied sustainable glass, tile, and recycled material nodes throughout. Added a winding river, cherry blossom trees, and green corridors. Refined lighting and scale across all districts.
04
Render & Exhibition
Rendered all camera frames in Maya Arnold and compiled the fly-through in Premiere Pro. Uploaded to Sketchfab for interactive 3D viewing. Designed a printed brochure for the live NYU exhibit.
Executive Summary

The project in brief

City of Tomorrow is a speculative 3D world-building project that imagines urban life 125 years from now — after an environmental collapse drove humanity to build a sustainable city in the sky. The project was created as a NYU Tandon capstone over 15 weeks, combining narrative design, 3D modeling in Maya, Arnold rendering, and a live showcase exhibition.

The central design question was: if architecture could encode a civilization's values, what would a post-crisis city look like? The answer became a city where every building is shaped like an everyday object — each one a monument to a different aspect of human life that was nearly lost.

Core Thesis: Sustainable cities of the future don't have to be sterile or utilitarian. By embedding cultural meaning into architecture itself, a city can be both technically innovative and deeply human.

The Challenge
How do you design a believable future city that addresses real environmental concerns while remaining visually compelling and narratively rich?
The Approach
Ground the world in a specific historical event ("The Great Shift"), then let every design decision flow from that narrative — materials, architecture, urban layout, even the brochure copy.
The Outcome
A fully realized 3D environment presented at NYU's Senior Showcase — with a fly-through video, interactive Sketchfab model, and printed brochure that together tell the complete story of the city.
Design Evaluation

What worked, what didn't

Evaluated against the original project goals: narrative clarity, visual coherence, technical execution, and real-world presentation impact.

↑
Narrative Strength
The "Great Shift" backstory gave every design decision a reason to exist. Reviewers at the showcase responded strongly to the world-building — the city felt like it had a real history.
↑
Symbolic Architecture
Using everyday objects as building forms (bread loaf, dumbbell, wine glass) made the city immediately readable. Each shape communicates its district's purpose at a glance — no legend needed.
↑
Multi-Format Delivery
Delivering the project as a video, interactive 3D model, and printed brochure meant different audiences could engage at different depths — from a 30-second overview to a full interactive exploration.
→
Material Realism
The sustainable glass and recycled material nodes read well in renders, but some buildings could benefit from more texture variety to avoid looking uniformly clean — real cities have wear and patina.
→
Population & Scale
The city reads as a model rather than a lived-in environment. Future iterations could add human-scale elements — street furniture, vehicles, pedestrian pathways — to ground the scale.
→
Interior Spaces
All buildings are exterior-only. The pod housing model shown separately hints at rich interior possibilities — exploring interiors would deepen the world-building significantly.
Recommendations

If I built it again

Three things I would do differently or take further in a next iteration of City of Tomorrow.

Recommendation 01 — Real-Time Render
Move the city into a real-time engine like Unreal Engine 5 to enable fully interactive walkthroughs rather than a pre-rendered video. Visitors could explore at their own pace rather than following a fixed camera path.
Recommendation 02 — Inhabited Details
Add human-scale elements — crowds, vehicles, dynamic lighting cycles (day/night) — to make the city feel populated and alive rather than a pristine architectural model.
Recommendation 03 — Extended Narrative
Develop a longer-form narrative companion piece — a fictional city guide or documentary — that uses the brochure's established voice to give every district and building a fuller story and cultural context.

City of Tomorrow started as a technical project — build a 3D model — and became a world-building exercise. The strongest learning was that the narrative came first: once "The Great Shift" existed as a premise, every modeling decision had a reason behind it.

The most visited stop at the NYU showcase was the architectural symbolism section of the brochure — people wanted to know what each building meant. That feedback confirmed the thesis: meaning embedded in form is more memorable than form alone.

Key Takeaway: World-building is a design discipline. The strongest environments — in games, film, architecture — are ones where form and meaning are inseparable. That's what City of Tomorrow was trying to prove.

The city's founders made every structure a symbol — a reminder of the values the city was built on and the balance between survival and joy. Each building in the Cultural District takes the shape of an everyday object chosen to reflect its purpose.
