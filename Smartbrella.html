Overview

An umbrella that listens
— and connects

SmartBrella is an automated umbrella that opens and closes in response to a sound command — specifically, the sound of a clap. It's also Bluetooth-connected to a companion app, giving users remote control and weather-based reminders.

The project was built using Computer-Aided Design (CAD), Python, and Arduino — with hardware components including VEX motors, a KY-038 sound sensor, a Bluefruit Bluetooth module, and an Arduino UNO board. Funded by the NYU Tandon EG1003 course, SmartBrella was a group project in which I served as Project Manager and Programmer.

The result is a functional smart umbrella prototype that automatically opens and closes via sound recognition or smartphone Bluetooth — designed with accessibility at its core.

Our Goal

Accessibility
& convenience

Around 15% of people in the world have disabilities. Of these, 28% are blind or have vision impairment, and 17 million are amputees. Simple everyday actions — like opening an umbrella — can require significant strength and dexterity that many people simply don't have.

SmartBrella's goal was to make the umbrella — an item everyone owns — accessible to people with paraplegia, amputation, Parkinson's disease, blindness, or similar impairments. By automating the open/close mechanism with sound recognition and a Bluetooth app, we aimed to build something that genuinely improves daily life.

The companion app took this further: not only can users open and close the umbrella remotely, but the app connects to weather APIs and reminds users to bring their umbrella when rain is forecast.

Process

From concept sketch
to working prototype

The project ran from February to April 2022, documented weekly in the team's engineering notebook. Three parallel workstreams — mechanical design, electronics and programming, and app/Bluetooth — progressed simultaneously, with the team meeting every Thursday at 2pm and dividing tasks by expertise.

01
Mechanical Design
Designed a pulley system with two VEX motors on a shared axle — drilled through a LEGO gear to feed the yarn string. CAD modeling in Fusion 360 produced an .STL for 3D printing. The steel cord was looped through the umbrella's internal frame. Two motors were ultimately needed to overcome the umbrella's spring tension.
02
Electronics & Arduino
Wired the KY-038 sound sensor, L293D motor driver, and Arduino UNO on a breadboard. Erica wired the Bluefruit LE UART module and established the first successful Bluetooth connection to the phone on April 21. The Arduino script detects a clap as a LOW signal and toggles motor direction — tracking last state to alternate open and close.
03
Bluetooth & App
A Bluefruit Bluetooth module was wired onto the breadboard, enabling the companion app to send open/close messages to the motors wirelessly. The app was designed in Figma with remote control, Bluetooth pairing, and weather API integration for rain reminders.

Hardware Components

What's inside
SmartBrella

SmartBrella combines mechanical, electronic, and wireless components — all assembled and attached to a standard umbrella using the available materials including duct tape, cardboard, and 3D-printed parts.

⚙️
Pulley Mechanism
2× VEX Motors · LEGO Gear · Metal Axle · Shaft Collars · Yarn String
Two motors share one axle with a gear at center. The string winds around the gear to pull the umbrella open, and unwinds to let it close — doubling the available torque by linking both motors.
🔊
Sound Recognition
KY-038 Sound Sensor · Arduino UNO · Breadboard
The KY-038 microphone detects a clap as a LOW signal. The Arduino script interprets this signal and toggles the motor direction — tracking whether the last action was open or close to determine what to do next.
📡
Bluetooth Module
Bluefruit Bluetooth Module · DC Motor Driver Controller
The Bluefruit module connects to the companion app via Bluetooth, relaying open/close commands from the phone to the motor driver — enabling fully wireless control without a physical interaction with the umbrella.
💻
Code
Arduino IDE · Adafruit BLE Library · Standard Arduino Library
Written in the Arduino IDE using the Adafruit BLE library for Bluetooth and the standard Arduino library for hardware control. The script recognizes clap inputs and Bluetooth messages, decides motor direction, and manages state across open/close cycles.

Challenges

What we ran into
— and how we solved it

Building a physical prototype from scratch meant encountering real engineering problems. Three major challenges shaped the final design: motor torque limitations, sound sensor sensitivity, and the handle enclosure.

Each challenge was iterated on until a workable solution was found — not always the most elegant, but functional and instructive for future iterations.

Motor Torque
The VEX motors couldn't generate enough force to overcome the umbrella's springs. When the required string tension exceeded motor capacity, the motors simply locked up. Solution: two motors were connected via a shared axle, doubling the available torque.
Sound Sensor Sensitivity
The KY-038 was extremely sensitive to physical touch — any contact would trigger it as if a loud sound had been detected, while actual claps nearby weren't always recognized. Adjusting the sensitivity threshold alleviated the issue, though not completely.
Handle Enclosure
Finding a way to package the mechanical components and wiring inside the handle was difficult. 3D-modeled and laser-cut containers were considered. Ultimately, duct tape and cardboard were used — a practical solution that kept the project moving within the course timeline.
Future Improvements
A production version would replace duct tape and cardboard with a properly designed 3D-printed enclosure, use higher-torque motors, and implement more robust sound detection using machine learning to reliably distinguish claps from ambient noise.
Executive Summary

The project in brief

SmartBrella is an accessibility-driven physical computing project — an automated umbrella that opens and closes via sound recognition (a clap) and Bluetooth smartphone control. Built by a four-person team over one semester for NYU Tandon's EG1003 course, SmartBrella combines mechanical engineering, Arduino programming, and app design into a single functional prototype.

The central design question was: how do you make the simplest everyday action — opening an umbrella — accessible to people who lack the strength or dexterity to do it manually? The answer was a dual-input system: sound recognition for hands-free use, and a Bluetooth companion app for more controlled situations.

Core Thesis: Assistive technology doesn't need to be medical-grade or expensive to make a real difference. Combining off-the-shelf components with thoughtful engineering can make everyday objects accessible to people who currently can't use them.

Next Steps

Evaluation & version 2.0

An honest look at what SmartBrella achieved, where it fell short, and what a second iteration would fix.

What worked
↑
Dual Input System
Having both sound recognition and Bluetooth control meant the umbrella remained usable even when one input failed — a redundancy that proved essential given the sound sensor's inconsistency.
↑
Bluetooth Integration
The Bluefruit LE module connected reliably to the companion app. The Open/Close/Weather interface was clean and functional — a strong foundation for a real product's control layer.
↑
Team Execution
Weekly meetings, clear task distribution by skill, and a documented engineering notebook kept the project on track across a complex multi-workstream build.
What needs work
→
Motor Torque
The two-motor solution worked but was a workaround. A production version would use a single higher-torque motor, reducing mechanical complexity and failure points.
→
Sound Reliability
The KY-038 was inconsistent — sensitive to touch, less sensitive to actual claps. This is the biggest reliability gap and the first thing to address in a next iteration.
→
Physical Packaging
Duct tape and cardboard worked as a functional enclosure but wouldn't survive regular use. A 3D-printed handle remains the right next step for durability.
Version 2.0
01 — ML Sound Detection
Replace the KY-038 with a microphone running a trained ML model (e.g., TensorFlow Lite) that reliably distinguishes claps from ambient noise — transforming the most unreliable component into the most robust one.
02 — 3D-Printed Enclosure
Design and print a proper handle enclosure that integrates the electronics cleanly. The CAD models already exist — a second-semester iteration would complete this step.
03 — Real-Time Weather API
Connect the app's weather feature to a live API so rain reminders are triggered by real forecast data — completing the full intended functionality rather than just demonstrating the UI.

SmartBrella proved that accessibility-focused engineering doesn't require specialized hardware or large budgets. The entire prototype was built from off-the-shelf components for under $100 — the barrier to assistive technology is design and engineering effort, not cost.

Key Takeaway: A working prototype that does 80% of what you intended is more valuable than a perfect design that never ships. SmartBrella opens and closes on a clap. That works. Everything else is version 2.
